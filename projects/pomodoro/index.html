Pomodoro Technique

A webapp to facilitate undergraduate focus and homework completion.

Pomodoro Technique is based on the strategy by Francesco Cirillo that divides unwieldy study sessions into more manageable blocks. Pomodoro (italian for tomato) represents each block and was named after the tomato-shaped kitchen timer he had used as a student.

Traditionally, a study session encompasses 8 blocks (or pomodoros): Start with a study block (25 minutes). Alternate between study and short breaks (5 minutes). The eighth and final block is an extended break (15 minutes).

Why use a Study Aid like the Pomodoro Technique?

Having an external cue can be an effective way to kick start studying: imagine putting on your favourite pair of running shoes at the gym versus some old runners in the lost and found. We, as humanbeans, can be conditioned to act a certain way based on environmental cues. Thus, behaviourally, having a cue you can access anywhere would help busy students study when they find the time.

This Pomodoro Technique prototype, specifically, has a few features that may improve focus. First, its minimalistic design prevents distractions and promotes students focusing on the task at hand. Second, the customization features allow students to fine tune the length of the study sessions and breaks based on personal preference.
